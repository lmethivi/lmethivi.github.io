Turbulent plume in liquid helium

Shadowgraph image of Rayleigh-Bénard convection (water)

Density gradients occur naturally at a natural state for example in the oceans, the atmosphere or the interior of stars. When they are stable, they support internal gravity waves. When they are unstable, they are at the origin of coherent structures called plumes. The development and the interaction of the plumes give birth to larges scales, turbulent motions that are mandatory to understand for the modelling of mixing, and energy transport in geophysical flows. However, for experiment in laboratory, it is difficult to raise more than few meters. They are often implemented using salt water (for internal waves), and water or air (for convection). Consequently, the control parameters obtained, such as the Reynolds number or the Rayleigh number, are far from those of natural phenomena. This is why we propose to develop experiments in fluids whose viscosity is very different from the one from natural phenomena, such as cryogenic helium and fluorocarbon. The use of cryogenic helium in cells convection has allowed to reach the highest numbers of laboratory Rayleigh, and to highlight some different regime.
